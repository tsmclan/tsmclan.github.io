It can be seen as a new way of organizing the drivers to participate in carpooling or ride-sharing, like doing spatial crowdsourcing tasks [28]. There are several spatial crowdsourcing platforms (such as DiDi, UBER, Gigwalk and BRUBHUB to name but a few) that provide drivers with monetary incentives in exchange for their efforts. A surge of research into CPD services have been developed to properly allocate tasks with the aim to either minimizing delivery cost (e.g., distance, time [1,2] and the number of selected vehicles [3]) or maximizing the number of transhipments [4-7]. Typically, the platform specifies the price per unit distance (or time) for the tasks and allots tasks if only their requesters accept the unit prices [9-11].

These methods have made progress to some extent, however, they still suffer from some limitations. The CPD is similar, but quite different from traditional delivery that relies on specialized contractors on markets; requesters in crowdsourcing markets typically outsource large quantities of simple tasks to uncommitted or even surrogate drivers. Actually there exists a great uncertainty on the CPD markets, but most methods optimistically assume that drivers will certainly accept the packages allocated by algorithms, rarely considering whether they are committed or not, whether they are satisfied with the fixed payment for their delivery efforts.

Designing effective pricing and allocation schemes presents a challenging problem due to requesters’ constraints and the realities of the CPD markets.

Motivated by this example, we formulate the CPD problem with Dynamic Pricing (CPD-DP) that aims to deal with dynamic pricing with the dynamics of the demand and supply on multiple local markets. To solve the CPD-DP problem, we ﬁrst propose a Delaunay Triangulation based (DT-based in short) pricing strategy to price each package with guarantees to set a personalized base price. Then we establish a reward model with the package’ s temporal and price constraints, and also an acceptance model based on the temporal discounting theory [14] to estimate drivers’ psychological reward.

Abstract. Crowdsourced package delivery (CPD) has gained great interest from the logistics industry and academe due to its signiﬁcant economic and environmental impact. A number of progresses have been reported, however, dynamic pricing, as a special and significant part of the uncertain CPD markets, is far less explored. To address the problem, we propose a novel Delaunay Triangulation based pricing strategy for dealing with the imbalanced demand and supply in local markets with the aim to maximizing the profit of the CPD platform. To cater to drivers’ wishes further, we apply the hyperbolic temporal discounting function to estimate their psychological reward in order to increase the possibility of package acceptance. We propose a three-stage framework consisting of the DTbased pricing, the two-phase matching pruning and the package assigning algorithm, to figure out the best-possible package-driver matching. With the dataset generated by Brinkhoﬀ road network generator in the city of Jinan, China, we have conducted a series of experiments to evaluate the proposed approach against several representative baseline approaches. Experimental results show that our approach significantly outperforms the other approaches in terms of effectiveness and efficiency.

To establish a sound incentive mechanism, we make the first effort to partition packages into a set of triangles by the well-known DT technique in order to find the tradeoff between the demand of packages and the supply of drivers. Also we use hyperbolic temporal discounting function to establish a package acceptance model that can estimate the psychological reward of the driver.

The package demand and driver supply are geographically imbalanced in distribution. Traditional fixed unit-price methods assume that the packages located in the same grid has same unit price. This is usually unrealistic since the demand and supply varies from a local market to another one. To solve this issue, we adopt the DT technique to divide the Euclidian plane into triangulation meshes, which capture the peculiar demand of packages and the corresponding supply of drivers, leading to a reasonable customized pricing. To do so, we select packages’ origins as vertexes to triangulate the plane. The DT of a package point set is the planar dual of the famous Voronoi diagram, which is a partition of the plane into polygonal cells, one for each input point, so that the cell for input point $p$ consists of the region of the plane closer to $p$ than to any other input point.

According to an introduction by Bern and Eppstein [28], if no four points lie on a common circle - as illustrated by the dashed circles in Fig.1, then each vertex of the Voronoi diagram has degree three, and the DT, which has a bounded face for each Voronoi vertex and vice versa, will indeed be a triangulation. In implementation, a triangular mesh generator rests on the efficiency of its triangulation algorithms and data structures. There are many DT algorithms, for more information on DTs and Voronoi diagrams, see the surveys by Aurenhammer [3].

Dynamic ridesharing is a successful and fast-growing area of the crowdsourcing landscape that provides a solution to reducing traffic congestion and parking pressure by sharing ones commute with other people.

Although partially easing the complexity behind the creation of ridesharing, these techniques still assume an oversimplified model of road networks. Typically, the straight line distance (i.e. Euclidean distance) is utilized regardless of the road network that are being used. It does not consider on which projection it is on, and it also does not matter what the distance the real route thinks that it has. So they turn out to be dispointing for rider-matching on road networks with roundabouts, one-way roads, or dual carriageways, and thus cannot be considered as a proper ready-to-use solution in real applications.

While Euclidean distance has been used as a heuristic to search for the closest POIs by their road network distance, its improvement on large-scale road networks has not been thoroughly investigated.

We propose to optimize real-time ridesharing with heuristics for road networks such as one-way roads and travel directions. These heuristics are embedded in a three-phased framework for real-time rider-matching 1. The first phase is pre-pruning, and the second phase is skyline pruning, while the third phase is the computation of shortest paths. In the first phase, we judge whether the driver is on the one-way road, then prune the driver through the calculation of the Euclidean distance. In the second phase, we use skyline computations in terms of price and waiting time to avoid providing riders too many candidate drivers satisfying all riders’ constraints.

We evaluate the proposed heuristics extensively using large-scale MNTG data sets. The experimental results show that, the size and accuracy of candidate drivers can be greatly improved after the prunning. Even with one-way road heuristic, our algorithm significantly maintains high matching accracy. The proposed heuristics improve matching accuracy and efficiency of the three-phased framework.

An ‘Deep’ and ‘Deep RL’ Framework for Task Arrangement in Spatial Crowdsourcing Crowdsourcing provides a convenient platform to collaboratively perform complex tasks. However, it is still an challenge for crowdsourcing schemes to assign complex tasks to the most suitable workers, for the difficulty to measure the skills quantitatively of every worker in large-scale tasks or situations need assigning tasks to workers who are the most suitable but unavailable temporarily. To this end, we investigate how to realize optimal complex task using Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL). Firstly, a deep recurrent neural network prediction model of workers-task matching degree is established to measure the skills quantitatively of a new worker, which is trained by historical task records of existing workers. Next, an DQN based RL framework is proposed to optimize the task allocation problem. In this framework, crowdsourcing problem is modeled as a sequential decision problem, where a DQN agent chooses whether to match the waiting task sequence and free workers at each time. The agent takes the waiting task sequence and skills quantities of free workers predictied by the prediction model metioned above as state inputs. Considering that the sequence length is dynamic, we add a LSTM layer to networks of DQN to realize the unfixed length of information input with fixed length of network input interface. Comprehensive experimental results demonstrate that our work significantly outperform related proposals.

Despite that Spatial Crowdsourcing (SC) is a promising paradigm for solving task cheaply via crowds of mobile users, task arrangement remains a signicant challenge due to highly unstable participants in terms of arrival and skill and commitment. To address this issue, this paper investigates how to realize uncertain descion making using Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL). Firstly, a deep recurrent neural network prediction model of workers-task matching degree is established to measure the skills quantitatively of a new worker, which is trained by historical task records of existing workers. Next, an DQN based RL framework is proposed to optimize the task allocation problem. Unlike previous deterministic optimization and supervised learning techniques, SC is modeled as a sequential decision problem in our framework, where a DQN agent chooses whether to match the waiting task sequence and free workers at each time. The agent takes the waiting task sequence and skills quantities of free workers predictied by the prediction model metioned above as state inputs. Considering that the sequence length is dynamic, we add a LSTM layer to networks of DQN to realize the unfixed length of information input with fixed length of network input interface. Comprehensive experimental results demonstrate that our work significantly outperform the state-of-the-art on several problems and benchmark datasets.
